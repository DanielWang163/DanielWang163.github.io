The Red Badge Of Courage Essay

Henry Fleming has experienced many major changes in his mind throughout the book. In the novel The Red Badge of Courage by Stephen Crane, Henry Fleming is a soldier in the Northern army and he is a coward at first, and he fled two battles. However, after he sees his friend Jim Conklin die in front of him, his inner rage covers all of his cowardness, and during the next fight, Henry and his regiment are like “mule-drivers” and defeat the enemies easily. Then he became a real hero. Henry Fleming was a hero in three ways, Courage, Duty and Transformation. When talking about Henry Fleming’s courage, he was at first a coward. “His emotions made him feel strange in the presence of men who talked excitedly of a prospective battle as of a drama they were about to witness, with nothing but eagerness and curiosity apparent in their faces. It was often that he suspected them to be liars.” ( Page.20 ). This quote explains that Henry is hoping that everyone else is feeling the same way as him so if everyone else are all cowards, he won’t be blamed. “Again he thought that he wished he was dead. He believed that he envied a corpse.” ( Page.104 ). This quote shows that Henry is a total coward and 100% fear and probably the worst thought that Henry ever had. Most of the time Henry had a weird mind and was kind of silly but also makes sense . However, now, after seeing Jim Conklin’s death, “And, furthermore, how could they kill him who was the chosen of gods and doomed to greatness?” ( Page.136 ). This quote is showing that Henry is no long scared and fear of the battle, he now thinks himself as if he was “the chosen of gods” and his cowardness is now transformed into “dooms to greatness”. Impressive. With that feeling he fought with maximum courage and he even rushed for the Rebellion flag in battle. That is very impressive. When talking about Henry Fleming's duty, he was trying to escape the regiment at first. “But he instantly saw that it would be impossible for him to escape from the regiment. It inclosed him. And there were iron laws of tradition and law on four sides. He was in a moving box.” ( Page.33 ). This quote shows that Henry was at first trying to run away from his regiment but failed miserably. At that time of the book he was still scared and a coward. However, now, he is in the final battle and he suddenly found out that the ”gaze of his soul upon that other flag. Its possession would be high pride. It would express bloody minglings, near blows” ( Page.195 ). This is when Henry rushed for the Rebellion flag and captured it. His duty now was to fight for the North and he was rushing into the enemies recklessly. Henry has experienced so much transformations in throughout the novel. “He went slowly to his tent and stretched himself on a blanket by the side of the snoring tall soldier. In the darkness he saw visions of a thousand-tongued fear that would babble at his back and cause him to flee, while others were going coolly about their country's business. He admitted that he would not be able to cope with this monster. He felt that every nerve in his body would be an ear to hear the voices, while other men would remain stolid and deaf.” ( Page.29 ). From that quote it’s easy to notice that Henry is still child like so it’s easy to see his changes throughout. “There was the delirium that encounters despair and death, and is heedless and blind to the odds. It is a temporary but sublime absence of selfishness.” ( Page.162 ). This quote is the main reason that Henry fights so strongly in the final battle. So showing contrast to the previous quote Henry has transformed from a boy into a man. Henry Fleming might be coward at first, but all that he’s done made him a hero at last.

How The Age of Explorations Changed The Americas

The Americas Before the Age of Exploration:

The Americas were actually settled before Christopher Columbus arrived in 1492 CE; in fact, even the first Europeans to ever set foot in the Americas--the Vikings, who arrived in Atlantic Canada around 1000 CE but did not stay--arrived thousands of years after the first inhabitants of the Americas. Indigenous Peoples came to the Americas around 15,000 years ago, likely by boat, followed by people traveling across the Bering Strait, which at that time connected Asia and the Americas. There are a few myths believed about the Americas before the Age of Exploration. Many people think the Americas were a relatively empty place, and that the Indigenous Peoples who lived on these two continents had simple and similar lives. North and South America were not, in fact, “relatively empty places”. The estimated population of the Americas was around 90 million to 120 million. Indigenous Peoples spread out all over the Americas and became many different groups and established different civilizations; some did large scale farming, and some were hunters and gatherers. The Indigenous Peoples’ lives were not simple and their civilizations were much more diverse than the myths claim.
